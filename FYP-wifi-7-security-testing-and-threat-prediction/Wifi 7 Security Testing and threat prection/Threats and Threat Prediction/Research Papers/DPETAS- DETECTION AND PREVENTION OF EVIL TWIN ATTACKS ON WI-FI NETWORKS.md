- **Title:** DPETAS: DETECTION AND PREVENTION OF EVIL TWIN ATTACKS ON WI-FI NETWORKS
    
- **Authors:** Kwadwo Adinkrah-Appiah, Atianashie Miracle A., Elisha D'archimedes Armah
    
- **Year:** 2021
    
- **Summary:** This review article discusses the significant threat that Evil Twin attacks pose to Wi-Fi networks, noting them as one of the most prominent attack vectors. It highlights that vulnerabilities exist even when traffic is encrypted and that there are "current shortcomings in Wi-Fi security protocols". The paper's main goal is to address the identification and avoidance of these attacks. It introduces "DPETAS" (Detection and Prevention of Evil Twin Attacks System) as a conceptual framework to address attacker strategies and provide guidance for users.
    
- **Important Points:**
    
    - This is a review paper that synthesizes information on Wi-Fi threats, with a strong focus on Evil Twins.
        
    - It emphasizes that attackers use Evil Twins to intercept traffic and steal credentials, even from secure networks.
        
    - It explicitly mentions shortcomings in existing Wi-Fi security protocols.
        
- **New Technologies, New Findings, and Other Important Aspects:**
    
    - **DPETAS Framework:** The paper proposes a conceptual framework (DPETAS) that combines technical detection methods with user-centric prevention strategies and guidance.
        
    - **Holistic Approach:** Its contribution is the call for a holistic approach that includes understanding attacker strategies and educating users, not just relying on technical detection.
        
    - **Relevance to Wi-Fi 7:** This paper's high-level perspective from 2021 is valuable. It reinforces that even with new protocols (like WPA3 in Wi-Fi 7), the underlying threat of Evil Twins persists. For security testing, this implies a need to test the _entire system_, including:
        
        1. The protocol's resilience (e.g., to downgrade attacks).
            
        2. The device's technical detection capabilities.
            
        3. The user interface (UI) warnings presented to a user when a potential threat is detected.
